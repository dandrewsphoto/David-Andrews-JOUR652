e-Scooters: Sidewalk safe or road ready?

By David Andrews

Are electric scooters supposed to be ridden on the sidewalk or the street? Is one safer than another? Are riders required to wear a helmet? Who is enforcing all of these rules and where can riders find this information?

Any other vehicle has clearly defined safety regulations. Cars must ride on the streets, drivers must wear seatbelts, enforced by local and state police. Pedestrians walk on the sidewalk and may only cross at the designated crosswalks. Jaywalking is enforced by local police. But for eTransit, legal language has proven extremely vague in ruling where e-scooters and e-bikes may ride, and restrictions can even vary from city to state to township.

In their relatively short two year history, e-scooters have been synonymous with controversy. Tech companies opted to roll out scooters across U.S. cities without regulation in place. San Francisco passed a temporary ban on e-scooters in June 2018 until regulations were in place, citing a lack of helmet laws and haphazard parking that interfering with pedestrian walkways and handicapped ramps.

This rider behavior suggests basic operating information was lacking in availability, or simply was not enforced.

The botched rollout seems unlikely to happen on a college campus where every change is first critiqued and evaluated before implemented.

In August, the University of Maryland installed 70 VeoRide e-scooters, 70 electric assist bikes and 150 pedal bikes at designated docking stations around campus.

In an email statement, Department Of Transportation (DOTs) Assistant Director for Marketing and Communications Cara Fleck Plewinski says "In general, e-scooter operators and cyclists are subject to Maryland State Traffic laws, which indicates that you must ride on the road unless there's a local ordinance that allows you to ride on the sidewalk" That local ordinance is DOTs itself, which states that sidewalks should be used when necessary.

"We encourage those using bikes and e-scooters to always wear a helmet and travel via roadways,” says Plewinski. “Sidewalks should be used when necessary, and individuals should yield to pedestrians to ensure safety." By Maryland Law, however, no one over the age of 16 is required to wear a helmet.

Downloading the app

When users first download the VeoRide app, users are taken to a map and prompted to unlock a bike, and asked to grant the app permission for the camera to use the QR code reader before they are given any safety instructions. Users can click the question mark, and then click the "Vehicle How-to" option -- 2 clicks away from the home screen.

As shown in the screenshots below, users can select the type of vehicle they're renting, and swipe through the vehicle-specific directions. The app doesn’t mention where to ride the scooter, any helmet laws that are on the books, or any additional corresponding traffic laws users must obey.

If riders wanted more information, they could in theory delay their pending ride a few more minutes and visit the VeoRide UMD page:

This page doesn't explicitly state to ride on the sidewalk or in the street, but rather infers the street by mentioning to "obey all traffic laws" and to "stay in your lane."

In a May 2019 study, The CDC and Texas' Austin Public Health Department found that 1/3 of scooter-related injuries occur to first time riders. UMD DOT’s FAQ page does mention that first time riders are encouraged to attend a VeoRide instruction demo. These demos allow riders to try out the scooters before riding them on the street and advertise the ability to obtain a helmet, but the page doesn't mention where these demos are routinely held.

So how do UMD riders adhere to these guidelines?

In order to get a first hand look at transit behavior, I scoped out two of the busiest footpaths on campus and logged every transit rider on a personal bicycle, e-scooter and e-bike.

The first stop was the intersection of Regents Drive and Campus Drive between 8am-9am on a Wednesday morning.

As the main bus thoroughfare for WAMTA and University shuttles, this 4-way intersection just outside the DOTs office is a vital artery for navigating campus.

There were 105 transit riders between 8:13am and 9:13am on 11/7/19.

The most frequent mode of transit was a personal bicycle, accounting for 67 of the 105 riders who crossed the intersection. 53 of them rode on the street, 14 on the sidewalk. 53 of them didn't have helmets on, while only 14 of them had helmets.

The next most frequent mode of transportation were scooters. There were 20 e-scooter riders, and 1 personal, non-electric scooter rider. 14 of them rode on the street, 6 of them rode on the sidewalk. Of the 21 scooter riders, 20 of them did not have a helmet on.

The second spot observed was Campus Drive right in front of the Stamp Student Union Building between 1pm and 2pm on a Thursday. Every lane has construction barriers, with the section east of the Health Services building divided into a bus lane and a car lane.

Of the 43 riders total, 25 were riding personal bikes and 14 were riding e-scooters. Only 3 bikers were on the street this time, and 22 were on the sidewalk. 6 of the 25 cyclists walked their bikes across the intersection, which only one person did at Regents Drive in the first study. All but 2 of the 14 scooterists were on the sidewalk. Only one scooterist was wearing a helmet.

Other vehicles observed were electric skateboards (3), e-bikes (3) and mopeds (8).

What vehicles have been observed on campus?

95% of all scooter riders observied were not wearing a helmet, and 75% of all riders didn't have a helmet on, even though the UMD DOTs page strongly recomends all riders wear protective headgear.

Also, there are no helmets available to prospective riders at the docking stations. Two riders included in the study appeared to be first time riders, as they approached the docking station and spent about 5-7 minutes looking at their phones before riding. Once they had unlocked the e-scooters, they cautiously rode down Regents Drive on the sidewalk--no helmets in sight.

Where do people ride on campus?

The data shows that this ambiguity of where to ride goes far beyond just e-scooters. 85 out of the 105 riders rode in the street, but 23 riders "felt it was necessary" to ride those same 4 roads on the sidewalk instead of the street regardless of the ve  hicle.

Policing the Sidwalk

Timothy Pilachowski, a UMD math professor, has been fighting to ensure pedestrian safety from bike accidents long before e-bikes and e-scooters motored their way into the conversation.

Pilachowski agrees with the observational study that about 80-85% of people do ride on the street, but he's out to make sure that number is even higher as these vehicles with a max speed of 15-20 mph tango with pedestrians.

"On campus, the speed limit for vehicles is 20 miles per hour. So these bicycles are doing car speed, or close to car speed, on a sidewalk, and sometimes weaving in and out of pedestrians. To me, that's asking for a problem because pedestrians don't walk anywhere near that fast," Pilachowski says.

Pilachowski, who was featured in an October issue of the Diamondback, has become somewhat notorious with stopping bikers on the sidewalk and informing them to ride on the street instead.

He's even started polling students on if they've ever been struck by a biker on the sidewalk.

"It was 11% [yes] in one class, and 15% percent in another. If that could be extended to the whole campus, 15% of 45,000 people [who have been hit], that's a lot of people."

"These bicycles are doing car speed on a sidewalk. To me, that's asking for a problem."

30 lb scooters tangling with 1 ton cars may not be the best solution either. Daniel Cutaneo, a sophomore computer science major, shared a dash cam video of a scooter rider turning right onto the street, and into oncoming traffic. That oncoming traffic was Cutaneo himself, and the two scooters collided.

"When I'm on the roads, it kind of bothers me that these scooters break every single traffic law known to man," Cutaneo says, with his helmet sitting on top of his backpack. "And they think they're invincible. They think they don't have to stop at stop signs, they don't yield at yield signs going around a circle."

Cutaneo says scooters disobeying traffic laws in the streets is extremely dangerous, given the lack of protection scooter riders have compared to the cars that share the same space.

"You're going 20 miles an hour, you get hit by something, you're going to go flying."

Since DOTs made the change to their policy this year to allow scooters to go onto the sidewalks as necessary, Cutaneo says he thinks riding scooters on campus has actually become safer. "Before that rule, I would always stay on the street. When I was in traffic, I would be lane-splitting. Which is also illegal and a bit dangerous. Cars don't see you going between them."

So where then should eMotorists ride?

"There are a lot of places on campus where bike lanes could be instituted," says Pilachowski. While the infrastructure isn't in place for bikes and scooters on this campus, Pilachowski says says it very well could be.

"There are places that are very wide, you could paint bicycle lanes on the sidewalk and still have room for pedestrians."

DOTs didn't say if there were any plans to build bike lanes to accomodate the influx of transit options, but said they continue to monitor best practices to align with the university’s ever-changing community.
